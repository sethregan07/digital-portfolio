Humanity has not evolved enough to use advanced technologies.

The problem is not technology itself—but who controls it, and how it is used.

At the top, there are often people with strong narcissistic or psychopathic tendencies.

So the deeper issue is human nature—and its patterns.

Most human problems come from the way power structures are built.

They enable and incentivize certain kinds of people to rise.

And once enough of these people control the direction of markets and systems, others are left with very little choice but to follow.

In that sense, we have to recognize something:

Human behavior has not changed as much as we think.

Even after thousands of years, many of the same patterns remain.

So while technology advances—

the same behaviors repeat in new forms.

More sophisticated. More scaled. More difficult to see.

You can see this in culture.

Violence reproduced in movies. Fandom reflecting tribal mentalities. Consumerism and social media reinforcing identity and validation loops.

Pop culture, in many ways, becomes an expression of deeper narcissistic patterns.

Strategy, market dominance, monopolies—

these can reflect another pattern:

A willingness to achieve outcomes at any cost.

Moral boundaries become flexible. Ends justify means a deeper form of psychopathy.

These patterns don’t disappear.

They take new forms through new technologies—while keeping people engaged, distracted, or unaware.

Wars and global conflicts also shape behavior.

They create fear, instability, and dependence.

And in those conditions, people often cling more strongly to leadership—seeking direction, protection, or certainty.

What is inside reflects outside.

And what exists outside reinforces what is inside.

Unless we address what is within—

our patterns, our tendencies, our reactions—

we keep mistaking new forms, without making any real change.

We see trends. We see innovation. We see new systems.

But underneath, the same structures remain—driven by the need to control and influence. Because the nature behind them hasn’t changed—only the form has.
